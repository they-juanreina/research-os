# Code of Conduct

Research OS encodes a specific set of commitments about how human experience should be handled, synthesized, and represented. Those commitments don't stop at the edge of the code — they extend to how contributors treat each other.

This document describes what that looks like in practice.

---

## The short version

Treat people the way this project treats research participants: with care, with plurality, with an honest acknowledgment of your own position, and without collapsing their complexity into something more convenient for you.

---

## What this project stands for

The [`CORE.md`](CORE.md) framework names five epistemic principles. They apply to contributions and contributor interactions:

**Care precedes optimization.** Speed is not a virtue here. Don't rush someone toward a decision when they're still thinking. Don't optimize a conversation for efficiency at the cost of the person in it.

**Plurality is method.** Multiple readings are required. Disagreements in this community are usually a sign that two different positions are both seeing something real. Treat them that way before deciding one is wrong.

**Tenderness is rigor.** Thoughtfulness is a quality standard, not a personality type. Taking care with your words and your arguments is not soft — it's the work.

**Context resists compression.** "Just summarize your point" is sometimes a way of avoiding a complexity that actually matters. Resist the pressure to flatten nuance when nuance is what's at stake.

**Binaries require defense.** "You're either with us or against us" is not an argument. Neither is "that's just how things work." Assumed dualities are questioned here.

## What is expected

### In code, documentation, and skill design

- Follow [`LANGUAGE_POLICY.md`](LANGUAGE_POLICY.md): gender-neutral defaults, no assumed pronouns, justified binary classifications
- Apply the five CORE.md principles in your contributions, not just in a preamble
- Name what is lost by your abstractions — every compression has a cost, and being honest about it is part of the work
- Surface minority cases and edge cases rather than absorbing them into dominant readings

### In conversations — issues, PRs, discussions

- Address the argument, not the person
- If someone's contribution has a problem, name the specific problem — not the contributor's competence or character
- Use people's stated pronouns. If you don't know, use they/them. No exceptions.
- Ask before assuming — about what someone meant, about their context, about what they're trying to accomplish
- When someone names a lived experience as evidence, that counts as evidence — do not require it to be cited in a peer-reviewed journal to be heard
- Acknowledge when you've been wrong. The same standard applies to code and to arguments.

### Across difference

This project is used by researchers working in very different institutional contexts, languages, and cultural settings. Contributions and conversations will reflect that. Some things that feel like obvious defaults to one contributor are specific cultural assumptions to another.

When you encounter a contribution that doesn't fit your expectations: ask where it's coming from before deciding it's wrong. That's the edge & variance scan applied to community dynamics.

## What is not acceptable

These behaviors are incompatible with the project's principles:

**Epistemic dismissal** — telling someone their methodology is wrong without engaging with their reasoning; treating one research tradition as the default against which all others are measured.

**Speed-over-dignity** — pressuring contributors to move faster than is reasonable; treating latency in response as a lack of commitment; creating urgency that doesn't serve the work.

**Category collapse** — flattening someone's stated identity, experience, or position into a simpler category because it's easier to argue with; treating their complexity as an obstacle.

**Presumptuous correction** — correcting someone's language, approach, or tools without first understanding what they're trying to do and why.

**Plurality erasure** — insisting that there is one right reading of a problem; treating dissent as confusion rather than as a different-but-legitimate position.

**Harassment** — repeated unwanted contact, personal attacks, publishing someone's private information, or any behavior intended to make someone feel unsafe or unwelcome.

## Reporting

If something happens that violates this conduct — whether it happened to you or you witnessed it — you can report it directly to Juan Reina at the contact information in their GitHub profile.

What happens when you report:

1. Your message will be read in full
2. You'll receive an acknowledgment within a reasonable timeframe (this is not a full-time project; "reasonable" means within a week, not within an hour)
3. The situation will be investigated with the same rigor applied to research — not algorithmically, but with attention to context
4. You will be told what action was taken, unless telling you would compromise someone's safety

Reports are confidential. Your name will not be shared with the person being reported without your explicit consent.

If the situation involves the maintainer (Juan Reina), and you don't feel comfortable reporting directly to them, that's a legitimate limitation of this structure. The project is currently maintained by one person. If it grows to the point where that's inadequate, the governance model will be updated.

## When the conduct document itself is wrong

This document encodes one set of commitments at one point in time. It is not final. If something in it reproduces the problems it's trying to address — collapses a spectrum into a binary, speaks over experiences it should be listening to, assumes a context it shouldn't — that's worth naming. Open an issue.

The conduct document is subject to the same plural-interpretations standard as everything else in this project.
