# Skills Library Audit — 2026-02-28

> This audit examines the library across two axes: **technical structure** (file completeness, consistency, invocation patterns) and **UX research methodology** (coverage, rigor, epistemics, lifecycle gaps). Findings are rated by severity: 🔴 Critical · 🟡 Important · 🟢 Minor.

---

## 1. Global Architecture Assessment

### Strengths

- **CORE.md as shared epistemic foundation** is an elegant architectural decision. Rather than repeating principles per skill, a single load point enforces consistency. The "Audit Before Acting" and "Edge & Variance Scan" sections are particularly strong — they operationalize plural epistemology as procedural steps, not just stated values.
- **PIPELINE.md field-level handoff table** is rare and genuinely useful. Most toolkits describe phase sequences; this one maps exact output fields to downstream skill inputs. That specificity is what makes composability real rather than aspirational.
- **Quality gates on every skill** create a consistent contract for output review. The pattern is uniform enough to be internalized quickly.
- **Language policy as a standalone file** (LANGUAGE_POLICY.md) rather than a buried footnote signals that inclusive practice is a structural commitment, not an afterthought.

### Issues

🔴 **`foundation-model-capabilities-mapping` is not in the official skill catalog.** README.md lists 10 skills; this is the 11th. It has no entry in the skill table, no phase assignment, and is not represented in PIPELINE.md. It is effectively a dark skill — it exists, but the invocation model doesn't acknowledge it. Either add it to the catalog or move it out of the Skills/ directory into a separate tools/ or utilities/ location.

🟡 **No versioning system.** Only `heuristic-eval` carries a `version: "1.0"` field in frontmatter. Skills evolve; without versioning, there is no way to know if a downstream experiment was run against the current skill or an earlier iteration. Consider adding a `version` field to all skill frontmatter, or at minimum tracking a `last_updated` date.

🟢 **`planting-research-seeds` uses `TEMPLATES.md` (plural); all other skills use `TEMPLATE.md` (singular).** Minor inconsistency but breaks the structural pattern. No functional impact, but worth normalizing.

**success-criteria-tracking** — ✓ Strong. The "confidence = lowest of three factors" model is well-designed and unusual in its care. The root-cause requirement for failed criteria is methodologically valuable.

### Lifecycle coverage map

```
✗            discussion-    session-note-taking      journey-mapping           saturation-     querying-   planting-research-
             guide          issue-log                hmw-extraction            analysis        research-   seeds
                                                                               success-        knowledge   heuristic-eval (Ph4)
                                                                               criteria-
                                                                               tracking
                                                                               heuristic-eval
```

### Strengths

**The querying-research-knowledge → planting-research-seeds loop** is a standout design. When evidence is absent, the skill doesn't guess — it generates a structured seed brief. That brief directly feeds the next skill. This creates a closed epistemic cycle: consuming knowledge surfaces gaps; gaps become new seeds; seeds grow into harvestable evidence. Most knowledge management systems break at this junction.

**Saturation analysis** is the most methodologically unusual skill in the library — in a good way. The distinction between New / Variant / Confirmation theme types, the rolling 3-session average, and role-specific saturation curves are features that don't appear in standard research toolkits. This is where the library exceeds published frameworks.

**Heuristic evaluation's dual mandatory output** (designer action briefs + research seed briefs) enforces the principle that expert evaluation cannot be its own endpoint. This closes the loop between expert review and participatory research. The methodology is sound; the execution gap (phases 02–03 missing) is a structural problem, not a conceptual one.

**CORE.md's plural interpretations requirement** (≥2 interpretations for any significant finding) is operationally rare. Most research frameworks acknowledge that interpretations are plural; few mandate it as a step. This gives every skill an inherent hedge against premature closure.

### Gaps and concerns

🔴 **No affinity mapping / thematic coding skill.** The library jumps from raw session notes directly to journey mapping and HMW extraction. In practice, the intermediate step — clustering observations into themes, building an affinity diagram, open-coding transcripts — is where the most interpretive work happens and the most bias is introduced. Without a skill at this step, the synthesis skills implicitly assume the coding has already been done. This is the most consequential methodological gap in the library.

🔴 **No recruitment / screener skill.** Participant criteria appear inside `planting-research-seeds` (as a field) and in `discussion-guide` (as an input assumption), but there is no skill for writing screener surveys, defining inclusion/exclusion criteria, or evaluating participant suitability. Recruitment quality is a primary driver of research validity. The gap is especially notable given the library's care commitment — systematic bias in recruitment undermines everything downstream.

🟡 **No synthesis reporting / dissemination skill.** The library produces excellent intermediate artifacts (journey maps, HMW statements, issue logs) but no final synthesis report. In most research contexts, there is a deliverable that packages findings for stakeholders who won't read raw outputs. A synthesis reporting skill would complete the lifecycle-to-delivery arc.

🟡 **Issue log priority formula may have unintended consequences.** The formula `Priority = (Frequency × Severity Weight + Role Impact) / Effort` places high-effort items in the denominator. A Blocker (severity=4, frequency=5, role=+2) with High effort scores: `(5×4 + 2) / 3 = 7.3`. A Minor (severity=2, frequency=3, role=+1) with Low effort scores: `(3×2 + 1) / 1 = 7`. These score nearly identically. In practice, critical issues should not be deprioritized because they are expensive to fix. Consider treating effort as a separate planning dimension rather than a divisor, or adding a Blocker override that bypasses the formula.

🟡 **HMW priority formula places feasibility in the denominator.** `Priority = (Impact × Urgency) / Feasibility`. Same structural concern: a high-impact, high-urgency opportunity scores lower when it is hard to build. Feasibility is a valid planning constraint but conflating it with prioritization can suppress important design opportunities. Consider outputting feasibility as a separate column for human judgment rather than baking it into the priority score.

🟡 **Journey mapping has no guidance on when a service blueprint variant is appropriate vs. a standard map.** The SKILL.md notes the variant exists but does not specify the decision criteria. Researchers may produce the wrong map type for the context.

🟢 **No quantitative research skill.** Surveys, usability metrics (SUS, SUPR-Q, task success rates), A/B test interpretation, and analytics synthesis are absent. The library is explicitly qualitative, which is a valid scope choice. If that's intentional, it should be stated explicitly in README.md to prevent misuse.

🟢 **No competitive analysis / benchmarking skill.** The `planting-research-seeds` taxonomy includes "Competitive Intelligence" as a seed type, but no skill exists to execute it. This creates an orphaned category.

## 4. Epistemic & Care Assessment

The library's epistemic commitments (plural epistemology, care as method, anti-compression, binary justification) are consistently applied across skills. This is rare and significant. Specific notes:

**Consistently strong:**
- Every skill references CORE.md explicitly and uses its language (not just a nod)
- Language policy is enforced through quality gates in discussion-guide (pronoun confirmation)
- querying-research-knowledge's zero-extrapolation rule and prohibited language list are among the strongest anti-hallucination mechanisms in any research toolkit
- Minority findings and contradictions are required outputs, not optional fields

**Areas to monitor:**
- 🟡 `CORE.md`'s "Plural Interpretations" section requires ≥2 interpretations for "significant findings" — but the threshold for "significant" is not defined. Individual skills set their own bars (heuristic-eval: Severity 3–4; saturation-analysis: doesn't specify). A consistent rule would tighten this.
- 🟢 The care check in CORE.md asks "where are we optimizing speed over dignity?" but no skill has a specific mechanism for researchers to flag this during a session. A facilitation cue in discussion-guide, or a note-taking flag in session-note-taking (alongside `[HESITATION]`, `[CONFUSION]`), would make care operational rather than reflective-only.
