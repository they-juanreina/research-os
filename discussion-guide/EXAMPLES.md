# Discussion Guide — Examples

Two worked examples calibrating the skill's output format. Use these to understand what a production-ready guide looks like before generating your own.

- **Example 1** — 30-minute discovery interview (discovery, current-state, tool-agnostic)
- **Example 2** — 50-minute concept validation session (prototype task-based, usability)

Both examples are abbreviated: they show complete PART structures but compress facilitator speech to representative passages. A full production guide would be longer.

---

## Example 1: Discovery Interview — Work Order Management

**Session type:** Discovery interview
**Participant:** Field service coordinator, mid-size dealership
**Duration:** 30 min
**Session start:** 10:00 AM
**P0 questions:** How do coordinators currently track and prioritize open work orders? What causes delay or re-work?
**P1 questions:** How do they communicate status to technicians and customers?
**P2 questions:** What tools or workarounds exist outside the official system?

### PART 1 — Introduction & Setup

**⏱ Duration:** 4 min | **Elapsed:** 0–4 min | **Clock:** 10:00–10:04

**🎯 Covers:** Framing, rapport, session logistics

**💡 Rationale:** The first 3 minutes determine whether a participant feels safe to be honest. Tone here sets the entire session. Err toward warmth and low stakes. Never mention goals that could prime the participant toward a desired answer.

---

> Hi [name], welcome and thank you so much for making time today — I really appreciate it. My name is [facilitator name] and I'm joined by [note-taker name] who'll be taking notes so I can focus on our conversation. Just so you know, [note-taker] won't be speaking during the session.

> Before we start: quick question — what pronouns should I use when talking about you? And is it okay if I use your first name throughout?

[WAIT for response. Update pronoun in your notes now.]

> Perfect, thank you. So — today is really just a conversation. There are no right or wrong answers, and I'm not testing you on anything. I'm here to understand how you work day-to-day. You're the expert in the room.

> A few things before we begin: we'll be recording today's session — audio and screen — just for our internal research team. It won't be shared outside and your name won't appear in any reports unless you'd like it to. Is that okay?

[WAIT for verbal consent. If declined, confirm note-only participation is fine.]

> And one more thing — if anything I ask doesn't make sense or feels unclear, please just say so. That means I wrote a bad question, not that you missed something. Just stop me.

> Any questions before we dive in?

[WAIT. Answer briefly. Do not over-explain the research.]

> Great. Let's start.

---

### PART 2 — Warm-Up: Current Role & Context

**⏱ Duration:** 6 min | **Elapsed:** 4–10 min | **Clock:** 10:04–10:10

**🎯 Covers:** Role, scope, daily rhythm — P1 context

**💡 Rationale:** Warm-up grounds the participant in their actual current experience before any research questions begin. Questions are broad, low-threat, and answerable without preparation. This also helps the note-taker calibrate the participant's context for later coding.

---

> I'd love to start just by hearing a bit about your role. Can you tell me what a typical day looks like for you?

→ PROBE: What does your morning usually look like before things get going?
→ PROBE: Who do you tend to be in contact with most throughout the day?
→ PROBE: Are there parts of the day that feel more predictable versus more chaotic?

📋 NOTE-TAKER: Capture tools mentioned, people mentioned, and any friction signals in the daily rhythm description.

> And just to orient me — roughly how many work orders might you be managing on any given day?

→ PROBE: Does that number vary a lot week to week, or is it fairly stable?

> Thanks — that's really helpful context. I want to dig into the work order piece specifically now.

[TRANSITION to Part 3]

---

### PART 3 — Work Order Tracking & Prioritization (P0)

**⏱ Duration:** 12 min | **Elapsed:** 10–22 min | **Clock:** 10:10–10:22

**🎯 Covers:** How work orders are tracked and prioritized (P0); what causes delay or re-work (P0)

**💡 Rationale:** The core research territory. Questions move from the general ("how do you track") to the specific ("tell me about a time"). The incident-recall question is highest value — specific memories surface behavior that general descriptions miss.

---

> I'd love to understand how you keep track of open work orders. Can you walk me through how you do that right now?

→ PROBE: Is there one place you go to see the full picture, or is it spread across a few places?
→ PROBE: How do you know which ones to focus on first?
→ PROBE: What happens when two urgent ones land at the same time?

📋 NOTE-TAKER: Note any tools, systems, or non-system methods named (spreadsheets, whiteboards, paper, etc.). Mark with [WORKAROUND] if it's clearly outside the intended workflow.

> Can you think of a recent time when a work order was delayed or had to be done over? What happened?

→ PROBE: Where in the process did things go sideways?
→ PROBE: Was there a moment where you thought — if I'd known this earlier, I could have avoided that?
→ PROBE: How did you find out something had gone wrong?

⚑ IF participant describes re-work due to missing information: Ask: "Is that a common pattern, or was that unusual?"
⚑ IF participant describes a communication breakdown: Mark [COMMUNICATION] in notes and probe: "Who was involved in that moment?"

📋 NOTE-TAKER: Flag [RE-WORK] and [DELAY] moments. Note cause (information gap, communication, system, capacity).

> That's really useful — thank you. I have a couple more questions about how status gets communicated.

[TRANSITION to Part 4]

---

### PART 4 — Communication & Status Updates (P1)

**⏱ Duration:** 6 min | **Elapsed:** 22–28 min | **Clock:** 10:22–10:28

**🎯 Covers:** Communication with technicians and customers (P1); P2 workarounds if time allows

**💡 Rationale:** P1 territory. Moves from internal tracking to the outward-facing communication layer. Time is compressed here; if we've run long in Part 3, this section may be shortened to close on time.

⚑ IF time is short (elapsed > 24 min): Skip customer communication question; prioritize technician communication.

---

> How do you usually let technicians know where a work order stands?

→ PROBE: How do they let you know when something changes?
→ PROBE: Is that working well, or are there gaps in that loop?

> And what about customers — how do they find out about status or timelines?

→ PROBE: Do they reach out to you, or do you reach out to them?
→ PROBE: Is there anything you wish you could tell them that you currently can't?

📋 NOTE-TAKER: Log communication channels mentioned. Note any gap between what coordinator knows and what they can share.

> That's really helpful. I just want to make sure I haven't missed anything before we wrap up.

[TRANSITION to Part 5]

---

### PART 5 — Debrief & Close

**⏱ Duration:** 2 min | **Elapsed:** 28–30 min | **Clock:** 10:28–10:30

**🎯 Covers:** Anything we missed; final participant question

**💡 Rationale:** Debrief creates space for what the participant thought we'd ask but didn't. Often produces the most candid moments of the session.

---

> We're coming up on time — is there anything about how you manage work orders that you thought I'd ask about, but didn't?

[WAIT. Give space. Do not rush this moment.]

> Last question: if you could change one thing about how work orders are managed today — not necessarily a technology change, just anything — what would it be?

[WAIT. Do not prompt or suggest. This question is deliberately open.]

> Thank you so much. This has been genuinely useful. I really appreciate you sharing so openly.

[IF RECORDING]: I'll stop the recording now. [STOP RECORDING]

> We'll follow up with findings when they're ready. Is it okay if we reach out if we have quick follow-up questions?

[WAIT for response. Note preference.]

> Take care and have a great rest of your day.

📋 NOTE-TAKER: Note session end time. Add any observations about participant energy, hesitation moments, or topics where they seemed most/least comfortable. These are not findings — they are facilitation notes.

## Example 2: Concept Validation Session — Technician Mobile App (Excerpts)

**Session type:** Concept validation / usability test
**Participant:** Field technician, ~5 years experience
**Duration:** 50 min
**Session start:** 2:00 PM
**P0 questions:** Can technicians complete a work order status update using the proposed mobile flow? Where does comprehension break down?
**P1 questions:** Does the information hierarchy match what technicians need most in the field?
**P2 questions:** Reactions to notification design.

> **Note:** This example shows Part 4 (a prototype task) in full detail and Part 5 (debrief) in outline. Parts 0–3 follow the same structure as Example 1 with appropriate session-type framing.

---

### PART 4 — Task 1: Update a Work Order Status (P0)

**⏱ Duration:** 15 min | **Elapsed:** 18–33 min | **Clock:** 02:18–02:33

**🎯 Covers:** Can the participant find and complete a status update? Where does comprehension break down? (P0)

**💡 Rationale:** The most critical task in the session. Status updates are the highest-frequency action in the workflow; if this fails, everything downstream fails. We observe without assistance — not to create frustration, but because the goal is to see the real behavior, not the coached behavior.

---

[SHARE SCREEN or hand device. Confirm participant can see clearly.]

[OPEN prototype to home screen — before any action is taken.]

> Okay, I'm going to give you a scenario. There's no trick here — just imagine this is a real situation.

> **Scenario:** You've just finished replacing a hydraulic pump on a customer's tractor. The job took about three hours. Your next task is to update the work order to show the job is complete and add a note about the parts you used. Go ahead and do that — just talk me through what you're thinking as you go.

[DO NOT assist. Do not point to anything on screen.]
[DO NOT say "try this" or "what does that button do" — wait for the participant to act or articulate confusion.]

📋 NOTE-TAKER: Log each tap/click in sequence. Mark [HESITATION] when participant pauses without acting for >3 seconds. Mark [ERROR] if participant taps the wrong element. Mark [CONFUSION] if participant verbalizes uncertainty. Timestamp each.

→ PROBE (if participant goes silent): What's going through your mind right now?
→ PROBE (if participant succeeds quickly): Was anything harder than you expected, or did it all feel straightforward?
→ PROBE (if participant gets stuck): Where would you expect that to be, based on how you use similar tools?

⚑ IF participant cannot complete within 8 min: Say: "Let me show you where that is — I want to make sure we have time for the next scenario." Note [TASK FAILURE] and timestamp.
⚑ IF participant completes successfully: Ask: "On a scale of 1 to 5, how confident are you that the update actually went through?"

📋 NOTE-TAKER: Mark [TASK COMPLETE] or [TASK INCOMPLETE] at end. Note time on task.

> Great. Before we move on — anything about that interaction that felt off, or worked exactly as you expected?

[WAIT. Let the participant reflect. Do not suggest answers.]

[TRANSITION to Task 2 or debrief depending on time.]

---

### PART 6 — Debrief & Concept Reaction (P0, P1, P2)

**⏱ Duration:** 10 min | **Elapsed:** 43–53 min | **Clock:** 02:43–02:53

**🎯 Covers:** Overall concept reaction; information hierarchy; notification design (P0, P1, P2)

**💡 Rationale:** Task-based sessions sometimes miss the participant's overall reaction to the concept as a whole. This debrief captures what the tasks couldn't — the participant's mental model of the product, what they'd tell a colleague about it, and what they'd want changed.

---

> Now that you've had a chance to try it — if you had to describe this app to a colleague who hadn't seen it, what would you say?

→ PROBE: What would you tell them it's for?
→ PROBE: Would you say it'd make their day easier or harder?

> Is the information on the main screen what you'd actually need to see when you arrive at a job?

→ PROBE: What's missing?
→ PROBE: What's there that you wouldn't use?

> [IF TIME ALLOWS] One last thing — I want to show you a couple of different notification styles and hear your reaction.

[SHOW Notification Concept A and B side by side.]

> No right answer here. Just — what's your first reaction?

→ PROBE: Which feels more like something you'd actually pay attention to in the field?

📋 NOTE-TAKER: Note exact language used to describe the concept. Log any implicit mental models in how participant explains the product back.

> Thank you — this has been incredibly helpful. We'll be making sense of all of this and I'll make sure the team hears what you shared. Any final questions for me?

[STOP RECORDING. Confirm follow-up contact preference.]

## What These Examples Demonstrate

| Convention | Where shown |
|------------|-------------|
| Pronoun confirmation before starting | Example 1, PART 1 |
| Warm-up questions framed in present tense, prototype-agnostic | Example 1, PART 2 |
| P0 territory receives the most time | Example 1, PART 3 (12 min of 30) |
| Conditional paths with `⚑ IF` | Example 1, PART 3 and PART 4 |
| Note-taker instructions (`📋`) separated from facilitator speech | Both examples |
| Probes labeled optional and listed as alternatives, not sequence | All parts |
| Task framed as scenario, not command | Example 2, PART 4 |
| Non-assistance rule stated in stage directions | Example 2, PART 4 |
| Task fail-safe with time limit and graceful exit | Example 2, PART 4 |
| Transition prompt closes every part | All transitions |
| Debrief leaves space for what wasn't asked | Example 1, PART 5; Example 2, PART 6 |
