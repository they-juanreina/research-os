# Discussion Guide — Methodology Reference

## 1. Why a Scripted Guide (Not a Question List)

A question list gives the facilitator topics. A teleprompter gives the facilitator words. The difference matters especially when:

- **The facilitator is not an experienced researcher.** Writing every sentence removes the cognitive load of improvisation, letting the facilitator focus entirely on listening and observing.
- **Multiple facilitators run the same study.** Consistency across sessions is a data quality issue. A scripted guide reduces interviewer variance.
- **The topic is technically complex or emotionally sensitive.** A thoughtfully phrased opening question is not the same as a well-intentioned but leading one. Scripting forces precision.
- **The session is being recorded and reviewed.** A written guide lets reviewers assess whether the facilitation itself introduced bias.

The script is not a cage — facilitators should deviate when a participant takes the conversation somewhere genuinely valuable. But the default is to follow the script and return to it. Improvisation is a bonus, not the method.

## 2. Types of Probes

A probe is a follow-up question that deepens or clarifies without leading. There are six probe types. Choose based on what the participant just said.

### 2.1 Elaboration Probe
**Use:** The participant gave a brief or surface-level answer.
**Goal:** Get more detail without steering.
**Examples:**
- "Could you say a bit more about that?"
- "Can you walk me through what that looked like in practice?"
- "What else comes to mind?"

### 2.2 Clarification Probe
**Use:** The participant used ambiguous language or referred to something unclear.
**Goal:** Understand exactly what they meant.
**Examples:**
- "When you say [word they used], what does that mean to you?"
- "Just to make sure I understood — you said [paraphrase]. Is that right?"

### 2.3 Specific Example Probe
**Use:** The participant made a general claim and you need grounding evidence.
**Goal:** Anchor the abstract to something concrete and observable.
**Examples:**
- "Can you think of a specific time when that happened?"
- "What did that look like the last time you had to deal with it?"

### 2.4 Emotional Probe
**Use:** The participant's tone, language, or expression suggested a stronger feeling than their words conveyed.
**Goal:** Surface the emotional dimension without putting words in their mouth.
**Examples:**
- "How did that feel?"
- "What was going through your mind at that point?"
- "That sounds frustrating — is that a fair read?"

### 2.5 Silence Probe
**Use:** The participant has stopped talking but seems to still be thinking.
**Goal:** Give them space. Do nothing.
**Method:** Count to 5 silently. In 80% of cases, the participant will continue. Resist the urge to fill silence with a new question.

### 2.6 Echoing Probe
**Use:** The participant said something significant and you want them to expand without asking directly.
**Goal:** Reflect the last few words back to prompt elaboration.
**Method:** Simply repeat the last meaningful phrase they said, with a slightly upward inflection.
**Example:** Participant says "I just gave up and asked a colleague." Facilitator: "You gave up and asked a colleague...?"

## 3. Managing Silence

Silence is the facilitator's most underused tool. Participants use silence to formulate answers, recover from an emotional moment, or gather courage to share something difficult. Filling that silence with a new question or affirmation destroys it.

**Rules for silence:**
1. After asking a question, wait at least 5 seconds before saying anything.
2. If the participant looks like they are still thinking, wait longer.
3. If silence becomes uncomfortable (>15 seconds), use a silence probe: "Take your time."
4. Never say "No worries" after silence — it signals the question was optional.
5. The note-taker's typing during silence is a feature, not a problem. The script should acknowledge this in the intro.

## 4. Handling Difficult Moments

### 4.1 The Participant Gives Socially Desirable Answers
Signs: They say everything is "great," "easy," or "no problem." They agree with everything you suggest.

**Recovery:**
- Ask for a specific example: "Can you walk me through a time when it didn't go quite as smoothly?"
- Use past tense: "Before this, how would you have handled it?"
- Normalize difficulty: "A lot of people we talk to mention that [X] can get tricky — have you ever run into anything like that?"

### 4.2 The Participant Goes Off-Topic
Signs: They tell a long story that is interesting but not relevant to the current part.

**Recovery:**
- Let them finish the current sentence. Cutting people off destroys rapport.
- Use a bridging phrase: "That's really interesting — and I wonder if we can connect that to [topic]..."
- Or: "I want to come back to that — but first, I'd love to hear about [next question]."

### 4.3 The Participant Asks "What's the Right Answer?"
This is common when participants feel like they're being tested.

**Recovery (scripted):** "There's no right answer — honestly, if this is confusing or doesn't make sense, that's the most valuable thing for us to hear. We're trying to make it better."

### 4.4 The Participant Criticizes the Design Heavily
This is ideal data, not a problem. Don't get defensive or explain design decisions.

**Recovery:** "That's really helpful — can you say more about what felt off there?"
If they ask whether you designed it: "I'm part of the team, but right now my job is just to listen — your reaction is exactly what we need."

### 4.5 The Participant Runs Out of Things to Say
Signs: Short answers, "I don't know," shrugging.

**Recovery:**
- Use a specific example probe.
- Switch to a scenario: "Imagine you had to do this tomorrow — what would that look like?"
- Try an emotional probe: "How would you feel if this worked exactly the way you hoped?"
- Accept that some participants have less to say and move forward.

## 5. Pacing and Time Management

The facilitator holds time. The note-taker does not.

**Pacing rules:**
1. The note-taker tracks elapsed time and signals the facilitator with a visible cue (e.g., holding up a number of fingers for minutes remaining in the section, or a pre-agreed chat message).
2. If a section is running over: skip lower-priority probes first, then skip optional tasks. Never skip P0 questions.
3. If a section finishes early: extend with a probe ("Is there anything else on this you'd want to add?"), then move ahead.
4. The debrief is the most commonly sacrificed section. Protect it. It contains the hypothesis validation questions.
5. Build in a 5-minute buffer at the end. Use it if needed; cut early if not.

### Time Pressure Signals (to pre-agree with note-taker)

| Signal | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 1 finger | 1 minute left in section |
| Open hand | Move on now |
| Thumbs up | On track |
| Circling hand | Wrap up this question |

## 6. Note-Taker Coordination

The note-taker and facilitator are a team. They should debrief for 10 minutes after every session, not days later.

**Note-taker responsibilities during session:**
- Track time and signal the facilitator
- Maintain the **topic tracker** checklist — tick items as they are covered
- Record verbatim quotes (not paraphrases)
- Log observation flags: `[HESITATION]`, `[CONFUSION]`, `[DELIGHT]`, `[SURPRISE]`, `[CONTRADICTION]`
- Maintain the issue log in real time (if usability testing)
- Do not ask questions during the session unless explicitly invited

**Post-session debrief (10 min):**
- What was the single most surprising thing?
- Which P0 question did we get the weakest data on?
- Any quotes that need to be verified against the recording?
- Any issues that need to be added to the log?

## 7. The Topic Tracker

The topic tracker is a checklist of every theme that must be covered. It lives at the top of the discussion guide and is kept open by the note-taker throughout the session.

**Format:**
```
[ ] Topic name — linked to PART N
```

**Rules:**
- A topic is "covered" when the participant gave a meaningful response — not just when the question was asked.
- If a topic remains unchecked with 15 minutes left, the facilitator must be signaled.
- P0 topics that are uncovered at the close of a section may be revisited in the debrief if there is time. Flag this explicitly in the guide.
- After synthesis, the topic tracker becomes a coverage audit: if a P0 was consistently skipped across sessions, the guide needs revision.

## 8. Framing Current-Experience Questions

Many discussion guides fail because warm-up questions presuppose the solution. A participant who has never used the feature being tested cannot answer "How does this help you?" They can answer "How do you currently handle this?"

**Rules for current-experience framing:**
- Use past tense: "Tell me about the last time you needed to..."
- Use tool-agnostic language: "How do you currently handle..." (not "How do you use UDP to...")
- Do not mention the feature name, the prototype, or the design until the relevant task section
- If the participant has no current experience: "What would you do if you needed to...?" is always answerable

**Good example:** "Can you walk me through the last time you recommended a dataset to a colleague?"
**Avoid:** "How do you use Collections to organize your datasets?"

## 9. Anonymity and Consent Best Practices

- Ask for recording consent before starting the recording, and again at the start (some tools start recording on entry).
- Offer anonymity as a genuine choice, not a default. Some participants want credit for their feedback.
- Revisit the anonymity question at close — participants sometimes change their minds after sharing something sensitive.
- Store consent in the session metadata. Do not keep recordings longer than your organization's data retention policy.
- If a participant withdraws consent mid-session, stop the recording immediately and note it.
